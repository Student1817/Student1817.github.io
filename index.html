The Mosquito's Relationship

Though many famous aircraft of WWII came chronologically before many aircraft which were mentioned in the “Modern Multi-role Aircraft Info Page”, one could argue that of them all, the DeHavilland Mosquito has the most in common with “Modern Multi-role Aircraft” or even the majority of modern military aircraft in general than some of the more famous birds of WWII (Spitfire, Lancaster, Mustang, etc.).

Just like the Mosquito, many modern aircraft have the ability to reach relatively high speeds. Though perhaps slightly more common in modern times, in the 1940s the Mosquito was unique (also the fastest allied aircraft) and this allowed it to use its speed to outrun opponents and avoid danger. This, though was useful at the time, is a lot more useful now that weapon systems are way more powerful in the modern age. The old doctrine of heavy slow bombers using turrets to shoot down hostile aircraft and using their size to reduce damaging effect from flak is no longer viable as a SAM or AAM will be able to down most aircraft if it can catch up to it.

Many modern military aircraft, both deemed “Multi-role” and not, have large carrying capacities. For Mosquito or Wooden Wonder as it was known, this consisted of a sizable bombay for ground or anti-ship bombs, points under the wings for more bombs or rockets and heavy forward multi-purpose machine guns. The Mosquito’s uniquely amazing ability to carry so much in a smaller airframe was due to its two powerful Merlin engines and terribly light wooden construction. In a more modern aircraft in the jet age, high carrying capacity means powerful engines and many hardpoints.

Hardpoints deserve some special focus however, since they are the reason that almost any hardpoint equipped aircraft can become “Multi-role” even if not designed to be. Because the modern primary air-to-air weapon is the missile (which use hardpoints) and all air-to-ground weapons also use hardpoints (though they did in WWII as well), an aircraft such as the F/A-18 can quickly have missiles or bombs swapped out on the runway to carry out a different mission. Or be loaded up with HARMs for SEAD missions, Harpoons for Anti-Ship missions and a targeting pod with Maverick missiles.

To conclude, the DeHavilland DH.98 Mosquito was both an incredible multi-role aircraft for its time (and also very beautiful) but it has inspired both design and combat doctrines of all modern military combat aircraft in one way or another. The Mosquito’s very similar characteristics to said modern aircraft really demonstrate this ancestry. Lastly, modern hardpoints are also extremely influential when it comes to the capabilities of modern aircraft and it is what allows them to be as adaptable and flexible as they are.
